## **1. Overview**

‚ë¥ **Player**: an economic agent participating in the game (e.g., firms, consumers).

‚ëµ **Strategy**: a plan describing what action each player will take.

‚ë∂ **Payoff**: the utility each player obtains as a result of the strategy choices (i.e., the game outcome).

> ‚ë† **u·µ¢**: the utility (payoff) of player *i*  
> ‚ë° **s·µ¢**: the strategy of player *i*  
> ‚ë¢ **s‚Çã·µ¢**: the strategies of players 1, 2, ¬∑¬∑¬∑, i‚àí1, i+1, ¬∑¬∑¬∑, n  
> ‚ë£ Typically represented in a table form, i.e., a **payoff matrix**.

‚ë∑ **Strategy profile**: the tuple (s‚ÇÅ, ¬∑¬∑¬∑, s‚Çô).

‚ë∏ **Game**: the set consisting of players, all feasible strategies, and payoffs.

‚ëπ **Game theory**: the theory of finding strategy profiles.

‚ë∫ **BR (best response)**

> ‚ë† **Definition**: for one player, fixing the other players‚Äô strategies, a strategy that maximizes that player‚Äôs utility.  
> ‚ë° A player may have multiple best responses (ties), so a BR need not be unique.

‚ëª **Nash equilibrium (NE)**

> ‚ë† **Definition**: a strategy profile where every player‚Äôs strategy is a best response.  
> ‚ë° In other words, no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their choice.  
> ‚ë¢ (Note) **John Nash**: winner of the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences; protagonist of the movie *A Beautiful Mind*.

‚ëº **Dominant strategy equilibrium (DSE)**

> ‚ë† **Definition**: a strategy profile where all players choose their **dominant strategies**.  
> ‚ë° A drawback is that it is not common in reality.  
> ‚ë¢ **Dominant strategy**: a strategy that maximizes a player‚Äôs utility regardless of the other players‚Äô strategies.  
> ‚ë£ **Theorem 1.** DSE ‚äÇ NE  
> ‚ë§ **Theorem 2.** Even if a dominant strategy equilibrium does not exist, a Nash equilibrium may still exist.
>
> > ‚óã **Reason**: the conditions for Nash equilibrium are weaker (less restrictive).

## **3. Sequential Games** (sequential game)

‚ë¥ **Overview**

> ‚ë† **Feature 1. Single-agent decision problem**
>
> > ‚óã **Definition**: a situation where one player can determine their strategy **completely independently** of what the other player does.  
> > ‚óã In a typical simultaneous game, a player is affected by the other player‚Äôs strategy, so it does not fit the definition.  
> > ‚óã In a sequential game, the first mover has the initiative, so it corresponds to a single-agent decision problem.  
> > ‚óã (Note) A simultaneous game can also be a single-agent decision problem if one side‚Äôs strategy plays no role.
>
> ‚ë° **Feature 2.** Unlike simultaneous games, sequential games distinguish **actions** from **plans of actions**.

‚ëµ **Example**

> ‚ë† **Normal-form representation of a sequential game**: may include unrealistic Nash equilibria.
>

‚ë∂ **Proper subgame**

> ‚ë† **Information set**: the set of nodes where a player could be.
>
> > ‚óã In **Figure 1**, Player 2‚Äôs information set consists of a single node.  
> > ‚óã In **Figure 2**, Player 2‚Äôs information set consists of two nodes.

>
> > ‚óã That is, an independent game contained within a larger game.  
> > ‚óã **Figure 2** has three proper subgames in total.
>
> ‚ë¢ **Feature 1.** Any game contains itself as a subgame.  
> ‚ë£ **Feature 2.** A simultaneous game has only one subgame.  
> ‚ë§ **Feature 3.** Since the root decision-maker is a single player, it is a special case of a single-agent decision problem.

‚ë∑ **SPE (subgame-perfect equilibrium)**

> ‚ë† **Definition**: a strategy profile that is a Nash equilibrium in **every** proper subgame.  
> ‚ë° (Note) For simultaneous games, the NE concept is relevant; for sequential games, the perfect equilibrium concept is relevant.  
> ‚ë¢ **Feature 1.** An SPE must be an NE.  
> ‚ë£ **Feature 2.** An NE need not be an SPE.
>
> > ‚óã **Reason**: proper subgames eliminate unrealistic Nash equilibria.  
> > ‚óã (Comment) Proper subgames can be seen as a method of solving via the extensive form, thus avoiding unrealistic outcomes.

‚ë∏ **Forward induction**: a top-down method to find SPE.

> ‚ë† **1st.** Find all NEs.
>
> > ‚óã In the above example, you can find (H, (HH, HS)), (H, (HH, SS)), (S, (SH, SS)).
>
> ‚ë° **2nd.** Check whether each is an NE in every proper subgame.
>
> > ‚óã (H, (HH, HS)): inappropriate because in the subgame following Hardware choosing S, Software chooses H.  
> > ‚óã (H, (HH, SS)): this is an SPE.  
> > ‚óã (S, (SH, SS)): inappropriate because in the subgame following Hardware choosing H, Software chooses S.

‚ëπ **Backward induction**: a bottom-up method to find SPE.

> ‚ë† **1st.** In the extensive-form sequential game, start from the leaves and move upward to find the first singleton information set.  
> ‚ë° **2nd.** Find the NE in each proper subgame.  
> ‚ë¢ **3rd.** Move up to the second singleton information set.  
> ‚ë£ **4th.** Find the NE in each proper subgame and mark it appropriately.  
> ‚ë§ **5th.** Repeat steps ‚ë¢‚Äì‚ë£ until reaching the root of the game.
>

## **5. Bayesian Games**

‚ë¥ **Overview**

> ‚ë† **Incomplete information**: at least one player does not know the payoffs for all possible cases of the other players.  
> ‚ë° **Bayesian game**: a game based on incomplete information.
>
> > ‚óã A mixed strategy game assigns probability to **actions**, whereas a Bayesian game uses probability to **infer information**.
>
> ‚ë¢ **BNE (Bayesian Nash equilibrium)**: for two players, a strategy profile (s‚ÇÅ*(t), s‚ÇÇ*) satisfying:
>
> > ‚óã **Condition 1.** Player 1, who knows t, must choose a best response.  
> > ‚óã **Condition 2.** Player 2, who does not know t, must choose based on expected values.
